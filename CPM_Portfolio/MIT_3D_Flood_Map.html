MIT's Communicating Flood Risk through Interactive Data Visualizations

MIT SUMMER RESEARCH PROGRAM URBAN RISK LAB SUPERVISED BY DR. MIHO MAZAREEUW AND RESEARCH SCIENTIST LARISA OVALLES (SUMMER 2023)

Effective flood risk communication is crucial for promoting resilience and informed decision-making in flood-prone areas. However, existing flood maps often lack accuracy and clarity, hindering their effectiveness. To overcome these challenges, we aim to create an interactive three-dimensional flood map of the MIT campus using advanced geospatial technologies and Mapbox GL JS, a powerful JavaScript library for web maps. By presenting flood hazards in a realistic and immersive manner, we strive to reduce scientific uncertainty in flood hazard assessment. The interactive flood map will enable stakeholders to better comprehend the true extent of flood risk, empowering them to make well-informed decisions and implement proactive measures for flood mitigation. Our research holds broader implications for architectural data visualization strategies and flood risk management. We seek to foster better understanding, engagement, and proactive approaches for flood risk communication. By providing a user-friendly experience, our work will empower diverse audiences to respond effectively to flood risks. Throughout the project, we worked on challenges in transforming 2D data into three-dimensional GIS data and selecting appropriate visualization techniques. However, we remained committed to enhancing the accuracy, visual representation, and accessibility of flood risk tto revolutionize flood risk communication through innovative data visualization solutions. By developing an interactive 3D flood map of the MIT campus, we hope to advance flood risk management and contribute valuable insights to the broader community.

Independent Part of Research

After a rewarding summer at MIT, I found myself eager to carry on with the project independently. My goal was to explore the potential of creating interactive emergency escape routes. It's safe to say that it remains an ongoing work and has evolved into a side project that currently holds my dedication.

Main Idea:

In a potential future scenario, amidst a flooding emergency, individuals will find themselves uncertain about where to seek safety and which areas are vulnerable to flooding. In this future, this tool can offer an indispensable solution. Instead of navigating the campus aimlessly and facing uncertainty, this real-time website can provide immediate access to precise guidance on reaching secure locations. Cutting-edge technology will seamlessly integrate the latest flood data, emergency response strategies, and campus layout to offer tailored escape routes. Whether one is a student, faculty member, or visitor, the system will be designed to ensure that everyone can swiftly and safely navigate to designated secure locations, minimizing risk and ambiguity during emergencies. By making informed decisions through a user-friendly interface, individuals will take control of their safety. The commitment is not only to enhance understanding of future emergency situations but also to empower everyone with the knowledge required to protect themselves and those around them. The vision is to create a safer, more resilient community by harnessing the potential of technology for the benefit of all.

Mock-Up

Important:

This mock-up serves as a preliminary exploration of the tool's practicality and feasibility in bringing my vision to life. It is essential to clarify that it does not provide an accurate representation of real escape routes. The data within the "2070 Storm 100-Year Layer" originates from the collaborative efforts of MIT's dedicated group of scientists. This dataset was compiled to support the MIT Climate Resilience Dashboard, which aims to furnish effective data visualization tools for presenting their findings. The escape routes I have illustrated in this mock-up are solely based on my personal interpretations, taking into account factors like water depth and topographic elevations. However, it is crucial to emphasize that these interpretations lack scientific validation and accuracy. To achieve a comprehensive understanding of flood risk, a more rigorous study would be necessary, encompassing not only the MIT campus but also the broader area of Cambridge. This comprehensive study should include all relevant buildings and potential refuge areas.
